# Nearshore Wave Transformation Modelling — State of the Art (2026)

**Question.** What are the current (2026) state-of-the-art approaches for nearshore wave transformation modelling — transforming offshore wave model output (global/regional spectral wave models such as WAVEWATCH III, GFS-Wave, ECMWF WAM) to nearshore and coastal sites? Cover: (1) dynamical downscaling with nested spectral models (SWAN, stationary vs nonstationary, unstructured SCHISM/WWM), (2) hybrid/lookup-table and matrix-transfer methods used operationally, (3) ML surrogate/emulator approaches (CNNs, neural operators, graph networks) trained on either model output or in-situ observations, including infragravity/long-period wave prediction, and (4) phase-resolving models (XBeach, SWASH, FUNWAVE) and when they're needed. For each: accuracy benchmarks, computational cost, data requirements, and operational-forecasting suitability. Conclude with which architecture best fits an operational metocean provider (Oceanum) that already runs per-site ML models (dual-input CNN, offshore GFS wave spectra + tide + SSH inputs → nearshore long-period-wave response at port berths, validated ~0.86 correlation at Cape Cuvier) and wants a generalisable nearshore wave transformation capability for arbitrary coastal sites, deployed as scheduled pipelines (containerised Python, TF/Keras) writing to a data platform.

## Summary

As of 2026, nearshore wave transformation remains anchored by dynamical downscaling with nested spectral models (WW3/WAM → SWAN, and increasingly single unstructured meshes in WW3 or SCHISM+WWM), which delivers benchmark accuracy of r≈0.96 and Hs RMSE ~0.2-0.3 m at nearshore buoys but at real compute cost and with skill limited primarily by the directional accuracy of offshore boundary spectra, not nearshore resolution. Hybrid/statistical transfer methods (sector-wise regressions, lookup tables) exploit the fact that static bathymetry makes nearshore spectra almost fully determined by offshore boundary spectra, achieving r≈0.90 at negligible runtime cost once a training archive of high-resolution model output exists. ML surrogates are the fastest-moving frontier: deep-learning statistical downscaling now maps full offshore directional spectra to coastal spectra without parametric assumptions (Gao & Jiang 2024), DeepONet surrogates of SWAN run ~three orders of magnitude faster with sub-1% Hs error against the parent model, and CNNs can even emulate phase-resolving SWASH physics (breaking, bore propagation) at laboratory scale — all trained on model output alone, but all region/bathymetry-specific. Phase-resolving models (SWASH/XBeach/FUNWAVE) are still required where phase-dependent processes dominate — surf-zone detail, harbour response, and infragravity/long-period waves — and nesting SWASH inside SWAN measurably improves nearshore Hs. For Oceanum, the evidence supports a two-tier architecture: an automated regional nested-spectral (SWAN or unstructured SCHISM/WW3) hindcast layer used once per region as training data, feeding a generalised spectra-to-spectra ML transfer surrogate (the direct generalisation of the existing dual-input CNN) deployed in the current containerised TF/Keras scheduled-pipeline pattern, with per-site phase-resolving runs reserved for generating training data at IG-sensitive port sites.

## Verified findings

### 1. Dynamical downscaling with nested spectral models is the mature, operationally proven baseline: nested WW3/WAM→SWAN systems achieve r≈0.96 against nearshore buoys (Norway 500 m SWAN-in-WAM; Karwar ~15 m depth SWAN-in-WW3) and Hs bias 0.01-0.03 m / RMSE 0.21-0.28 m in the operational Southern California NCEX system — clearly better than contemporaneous global-model errors (0.4-0.6 m RMSE) — but systematic biases persist (e.g., +18% / 0.29 m Hs overestimate at the Norwegian site).

- **Confidence:** high  |  **Adversarial vote:** 3-0 on all four merged claims
- **Evidence:** Merged claims 8, 11, 13, 24. Rogers et al. 2007: triple-nested SWAN (3 km nonstationary outer, 621 m and 39 m stationary inner nests) forced by NCEP WW3/GFS ran twice daily with 7-day forecasts during NCEX 2003; Table 2 shows bias 0.03 m / RMSE 0.28 m overall, 0.01 m / 0.21 m at inner nests vs NDBC/CDIP buoys. Breivik et al. 2009: SWAN 500 m in WAM 10 km, r=0.96 vs buoy, +18% Hs bias. Amrutha et al. 2016: SWAN nested in WW3, r=0.96 for Hs at 15 m depth over a full year. All verified verbatim against primary peer-reviewed full texts, unanimous votes.

### 2. Within nested SWAN systems, the stationary-mode shortcut costs accuracy (>12 cm RMS Hs error from mistimed swell arrivals in model-to-model sensitivity tests) while coarser resolution costs little (<7 cm); nonstationary computation significantly improved RMS error against observations and became operationally feasible via parallelism even on 2003-era hardware (7-day forecast: ~28 h serial → ~100 min on 8 OpenMP threads; stationary saves a factor 15-30). On 2026 hardware, nonstationary sub-regional nests are computationally trivial (a 2009-era Linux workstation already ran a 2-day 500 m forecast in under an hour).

- **Confidence:** high  |  **Adversarial vote:** claim 9: 2-1; claims 10, 14: 3-0
- **Evidence:** Merged claims 9, 10, 14. Rogers et al. 2007 conclusions verified verbatim; note the >12 cm / <7 cm figures are sensitivity statistics computed against the nonstationary high-resolution run as truth (Table 3), while against buoys the stationary penalty was described as 'modest'. Breivik et al. 2009 cost figures ('two-day forecasts completed in less than one hour on a standard Linux workstation'; 16-month climatology in under a week given archived boundary conditions) verified verbatim.
  - https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378383906000937
  - https://arxiv.org/pdf/1206.3055

### 3. The dominant accuracy bottleneck for nearshore prediction in island-sheltered regions is the directional distribution of wave energy in the offshore boundary spectra, not nearshore grid resolution: directionally biased boundary forcing (e.g., WW3 overpredicting SW swell while underpredicting NW swell) can look correct in total offshore Hs yet cause large nearshore bias, and higher outer-grid resolution produced little or no improvement against observations.

- **Confidence:** high  |  **Adversarial vote:** 3-0
- **Evidence:** Claim 12, verified verbatim against Rogers et al. 2007 full text ('the accuracy of directional distribution of boundary forcing is critical... It is not enough to evaluate the accuracy of boundary forcing simply by comparing significant waveheight'), independently corroborated by Crosby, O'Reilly & Guza 2016 (JTECH 33:1673: nearshore error in sheltered Southern California 'is often dominated by directional uncertainty offshore'). Directly relevant to Oceanum: quality of the input offshore spectra (GFS-Wave directional resolution) bounds achievable nearshore skill regardless of the transformation method.
  - https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378383906000937

### 4. Unstructured single-mesh models now offer a viable alternative to the traditional WW3→SWAN nesting chain: unstructured WAVEWATCH III achieves accuracy comparable to unstructured SWAN for coastal shallow-water simulation in swell-dominated waters, and a single global SCHISM+WWM-V mesh (651,652 nodes, ~50 km offshore to 2-4 km at the coast) delivers seamless global-to-nearshore waves and surge with Hs NRMSE 16.58% / bias -3.73% vs altimeters, degrading to 25.79% / -11.8% at coastal buoys; its skill gain over coarser uncoupled models is concentrated at the coast, attributed to resolving small coastal features and wave-current interaction.

- **Confidence:** high  |  **Adversarial vote:** 3-0 on all four merged claims
- **Evidence:** Merged claims 17, 18, 19, 20. Frontiers 2026 (UNWWIII vs UNSWAN at Hambantota, Sri Lanka, single-site but corroborated by Abdolali et al. 2020 and NOAA operational unstructured WW3): 'UNWWIII is fully applicable to coastal shallow-water wave simulations and achieves comparable accuracy to the widely used UNSWAN model.' Mentaschi et al. 2023 (peer-reviewed, EC-JRC): mesh, resolution, coupling, and validation statistics all verified verbatim. Caveats: SCHISM hindcast is ERA5-forced demonstration not operational forecast; 2-4 km is still far from surf-zone scale; the resolution-vs-coupling attribution is not ablated.
  - https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/marine-science/articles/10.3389/fmars.2026.1877867/full
  - https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/marine-science/articles/10.3389/fmars.2023.1233679/full

### 5. Hybrid/statistical transfer methods rest on a verified physical premise — coastal wave dynamics are dominated by static local bathymetry, so nearshore directional spectra are almost fully determined once offshore boundary spectra are known — and this same premise underlies lookup-table/matrix-transfer methods and Oceanum's per-site CNN approach. The premise is weaker for infragravity/long-period waves, where nonlinear group forcing, harbour resonance, and water-level dependence add dynamic degrees of freedom (consistent with Oceanum feeding tide/SSH as extra inputs).

- **Confidence:** high  |  **Adversarial vote:** 3-0
- **Evidence:** Claim 2, verified verbatim against Gao & Jiang 2024 (Coastal Engineering 192, 104557): 'wave dynamics are strongly influenced by the topography, implying that once the DWSs at the open ocean boundary are known, the DWSs at various locations along the coast are almost determined.' Verifier independently corroborated the same premise in the hybrid-downscaling literature (Camus et al. 2011, HyWaves) and flagged the IG-wave caveat explicitly.
  - https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378383924001054

### 6. Operationally proven hybrid downscaling: a direction-dependent (sector-wise) linear regression from offshore hindcast Hs to nearshore SWAN Hs reproduces nearshore conditions at r=0.91 vs SWAN and r=0.90 vs independent buoy observations — only slightly below the full dynamical model's 0.96 — and once a training archive of high-resolution model output exists (16 months of SWAN sufficed), downscaling a 52-year offshore hindcast to arbitrary points inside the domain has negligible computational cost; the main manual effort is choosing directional sectors per location.

- **Confidence:** high  |  **Adversarial vote:** 3-0 on both merged claims
- **Evidence:** Merged claims 15, 16. Breivik et al. 2009 (J. Marine Systems 78, S235-S243, Norwegian Met Institute), all figures verified verbatim against the full text, including 'This procedure can be repeated for any location within the SWAN model grid at extremely little extra cost.' Caveats: single semi-sheltered site, the regression inherits SWAN's +18% bias, and the highest waves are slightly underestimated. This is the classic template for the 'train once dynamically, transfer statistically forever' economics that also govern ML surrogates.
  - https://arxiv.org/pdf/1206.3055

### 7. ML statistical downscaling of full directional wave spectra is now demonstrated: Gao & Jiang (2024) built a deep learning model that computes coastal directional wave spectra directly from open-ocean directional spectra without assuming any predefined spectral shape (no JONSWAP/parametric partitions), explicitly motivated by the computational expense of dynamical downscaling with nested numerical wave models. The model is region-specific (trained on the Southern California Bight, exploiting fixed local bathymetry), so 'arbitrary coastal sites' implies per-region retraining rather than one universal model.

- **Confidence:** high  |  **Adversarial vote:** 3-0 on both merged claims
- **Evidence:** Merged claims 0, 1. Abstract verified verbatim via Semantic Scholar/Crossref (DOI 10.1016/j.coastaleng.2024.104557, Coastal Engineering 192): 'a deep learning approach that can compute coastal DWSs from open ocean DWSs... without relying on any predefined spectral shapes' and 'Dynamic downscaling methods reliant on numerical wave models are often computationally expensive.' This is the closest published analogue to a generalisation of Oceanum's per-site CNN: spectra-in/spectra-out rather than spectra-in/parameter-out, trained per region on nested-model output.
  - https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378383924001054

### 8. Neural-operator surrogates of SWAN deliver ~three-orders-of-magnitude speedup with near-parent accuracy: a DeepONet surrogate runs at ~0.04 s per scenario vs ~30 s for SWAN, and on a realistic steady-state Duck, NC test case predicts Hs with relative errors below 1% for most test scenarios (worst case 1.91%), with radiation-stress-gradient (force) errors reaching a worst case of ~11%. Errors are measured against SWAN output, not observations; the benchmark excludes training-data generation cost (~2,700-3,800 SWAN runs per case) and covers steady-state cases only.

- **Confidence:** medium  |  **Adversarial vote:** 3-0 on both merged claims
- **Evidence:** Merged claims 3, 4. Full text of arXiv:2604.06433 (Cai, Dutta, Loveland, Valseth, Rivera-Casillas, Trahan, Dawson — UT Austin / US Army ERDC, April 2026) verified: '0.04 seconds per scenario... nearly three-order-of magnitude speedup compared to about 30 seconds required by traditional SWAN simulations'; Hs RLE below 1.0% for the majority of 576 held-out scenarios, max force RLE 10.98% (x) / 6.88% (y). Graded medium despite unanimous votes because it is a single self-reported preprint, not yet peer-reviewed, with unspecified hardware.
  - https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.06433

### 9. ML can emulate phase-resolving nearshore physics from model output alone: Wei & Davison (Coastal Engineering, 2022) trained a CNN entirely on SWASH (non-hydrostatic, phase-resolving) simulations of a laboratory rip-current experiment to do next-frame prediction of water surface elevation and cross-shore/longshore velocity, and it accurately reproduced wave propagation over the offshore slope, depth-induced breaking over a sandbar, and bore propagation including crest bending and separation (~22.5x speedup over SWASH). This is a spatiotemporal field emulator, not an offshore-spectra-to-nearshore transfer model, and is demonstrated only at laboratory scale on a single bathymetry within the training distribution.

- **Confidence:** medium  |  **Adversarial vote:** 3-0 on all three merged claims
- **Evidence:** Merged claims 5, 6, 7. Verified against Crossref/Semantic Scholar (DOI 10.1016/j.coastaleng.2021.104044, Coastal Engineering 171) with abstract-level verbatim matches for the train/evaluate split on SWASH output and the reproduced processes. Peer-reviewed and cited approvingly by later reviews, but a single paper at lab scale — hence medium for the practical implication. Relevance to Oceanum: proof that surrogates for phase-resolving/IG-relevant physics can be trained on synthetic model output without in-situ data, but field-scale, multi-bathymetry generalisation remains undemonstrated in the verified record.
  - https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S037838392100185X

### 10. Phase-resolving models add measurable value in the final nearshore step: at four buoy sites on India's east coast, a WW3→SWAN→SWASH nesting chain simulated Hs more accurately than SWAN alone (higher correlation and index of agreement, including during Cyclone Hudhud), with the SWAN-only setup underestimating nearshore wave heights in that study's conditions; the authors recommend locally nested SWAN-SWASH for nearshore prediction. Note SWAN bias direction is condition-dependent (other sites show overestimation), so 'systematic underestimation' is scoped to this region.

- **Confidence:** medium  |  **Adversarial vote:** claims 21, 22: 3-0; claim 23: 2-1
- **Evidence:** Merged claims 21, 22, 23. Umesh & Behera 2021 (Ocean Engineering 236, 109550) verified at abstract level (full text paywalled): two setups (SWAN, SWAN-SWASH) with WW3 boundary conditions; 'a nested SWAN-SWASH setup should be adopted to locally advance the wave prediction in the nearshore regions, reducing the underestimation of wave estimates with SWAN only setup' (verbatim). Single-paper evidence with one 2-1 vote and abstract-only verification → medium. Practical reading: phase-resolving nests are warranted where phase-dependent processes (breaking detail, harbour agitation, IG generation) matter, and can serve as training-data generators rather than operational runtime components.
  - https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0029801821009409

### 11. Best-fit architecture for Oceanum: a two-tier 'dynamical-once, ML-forever' system. Tier 1: per-region automated nested spectral hindcast (nonstationary SWAN nests, or an unstructured SCHISM/WWM or UNWWIII mesh where currents/surge matter) forced by archived offshore spectra, run once per new region as a scheduled batch — computationally cheap on modern hardware (hours-to-days per region per the verified cost benchmarks). Tier 2: a spectra-to-spectra ML transfer surrogate (Gao & Jiang-style deep downscaling or a DeepONet-class operator) trained on Tier 1 output, conditioned on tide/SSH as Oceanum already does, deployed in the existing containerised TF/Keras scheduled-pipeline pattern at negligible inference cost (~10^-2 s per forecast step). The existing per-site dual-input CNNs remain the right tool for berth-scale long-period/IG response — the verified literature shows the static-bathymetry premise weakens exactly there — with phase-resolving SWASH/XBeach runs used offline to generate IG training data at new port sites rather than run operationally. Verified evidence also implies two design constraints: nearshore skill is bounded by the directional quality of the offshore boundary spectra (so preserve full directional-spectral inputs, as the current CNN does), and every ML tier needs per-region retraining, so the pipeline should automate the Tier 1 → training → validation loop per site.

- **Confidence:** medium  |  **Adversarial vote:** synthesis
- **Evidence:** Synthesis across all 25 verified claims rather than a single source. Supporting chain: statistical/ML transfer achieves r≈0.90 vs 0.96 for full dynamics at negligible cost once a training archive exists (Breivik); ML now does this for full directional spectra without parametric assumptions (Gao & Jiang) and for full SWAN fields at ~750x speedup (DeepONet preprint); the static-bathymetry determinism premise justifies per-region transfer models (Gao & Jiang, corroborated by Camus et al.); directional boundary-spectra quality is the binding error source (Rogers, Crosby et al.); phase-resolving models are needed for IG/harbour physics and can be nested or emulated (Umesh & Behera; Wei & Davison). Confidence medium because the recommendation is an inference — no verified source benchmarks this exact architecture end-to-end, and field-scale IG prediction by ML surrogates beyond Oceanum's own ~0.86-correlation result is not covered by the verified claims.
